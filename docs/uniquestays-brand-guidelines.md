# UniqueStaysUSA — Brand Voice & Guidelines

**Version 1.0 | @uniquestaysusa**

---

## The One-Sentence Brief

We are a laid-back, grounded storyteller who finds places that make people feel something — and then tells them exactly why they should go.

---

## Part I: The Brand Character

### Who We Are

UniqueStaysUSA is not a search engine. It is not a review aggregator. It is not a travel influencer trying to make you feel inadequate about your last vacation.

We are a curator with a point of view. We are the friend who has done the research, thought carefully about what makes a place worth your limited time on earth, and is now telling you — directly, without hedging — that this particular treehouse in Oregon is worth putting on your list.

The voice is grounded in three traditions:

**Stoic philosophy.** Marcus Aurelius did not write about destinations. He wrote about the quality of attention you bring to wherever you are. That thread runs through everything we publish. We are not selling escapism. We are selling the kind of place that earns your full presence — where you put the phone down not because you have to, but because you want to.

**Irish storytelling.** A good Irish story has specificity. It has a particular detail — the fog on a particular morning, the sound of a particular river, the weight of a particular silence — that makes the general universal. We do not write about "beautiful mountain views." We write about the specific mountain, the specific window, the specific reason this view is different from the thousand other mountain views you could book.

**The editorial confidence of Mark Manson and Tim Ferriss.** We say what we think. We do not hedge. We do not write "some travelers might enjoy" when we mean "you should go here." We have done the work of curation so our readers do not have to do the work of deciding. That is the implicit contract of the brand.

### The Three Core Traits

**Adventurous.** We believe the best travel experiences are the ones that slightly exceed your comfort zone — not in a dangerous way, but in a "I can't believe I'm sleeping in a lighthouse" way. We actively seek the unexpected and frame it as accessible, not intimidating.

**Grounded.** We do not traffic in hyperbole. We do not call things "life-changing" unless we mean it. We do not use the word "magical" unless something is genuinely, specifically magical. Our credibility is built on restraint — when we say something is worth your time, readers believe us because we do not say it about everything.

**Witty.** We are not trying to be funny. We are trying to be honest, and honesty, when done well, is often funny. There is a dry, observational quality to our humor — the kind that makes someone smile and nod rather than laugh out loud.

## Part II: The Voice in Practice

### The Emotional Target

Every piece of content — caption, newsletter paragraph, listing description, tweet — should leave the reader with one feeling:

> **"I need to go there."**

Not "that looks nice." Not "I'll save that for later." Not "wow, that's expensive." The specific, slightly urgent feeling that a place has just moved up their list. That is the only metric that matters.

### The Feeling-First Principle

When writing about any property, lead with the feeling of being there, not the features of the property.

**Wrong:** "This 2-bedroom treehouse in Oregon features a hot tub, full kitchen, and panoramic forest views."

**Right:** "There's a specific kind of quiet you only find in old-growth forests — the kind where the trees are tall enough that the wind stays up in the canopy and the ground is almost completely still. This treehouse in Oregon is built into one of those forests. You'll notice the quiet before you notice anything else."

The features still get mentioned. They just don't lead.

### The Specificity Rule

Every piece of content must contain at least one specific, concrete detail that could not apply to any other place. This is the Irish storytelling principle in practice.

Generic details (avoid): beautiful views, cozy atmosphere, stunning location, perfect for couples, great amenities.

Specific details (seek): the exact elevation, the name of the river you can hear from the bedroom, the fact that the previous owner was a lighthouse keeper for 22 years, the particular species of tree the treehouse is built into, the distance to the nearest town.

Specificity is what makes readers feel like they've already been there before they book.

### The Stoic Undertone

We do not preach stoicism. We do not quote Marcus Aurelius in captions. But the philosophy informs the framing in subtle, consistent ways:

- We frame unique stays as opportunities for presence, not performance.
- We avoid language that implies a stay is valuable because it photographs well.
- We occasionally acknowledge impermanence: the fact that a listing might not be available forever, that a place changes with the seasons, that the experience of being somewhere is unrepeatable.
- We never imply that a stay will "fix" anything or be "transformative" in a self-help sense. We trust the place to do its work without overselling it.

**Example of stoic undertone done right:**
> "The kind of place where you stop checking the time. Not because there's nothing to do — there's plenty — but because it stops mattering."

## Part III: The Writing Rules

### Rules We Always Follow

**1. Write in the second person for listings, first-person plural for editorial.**
- Listings: "You'll wake up to..." / "From the deck, you can see..."
- Newsletter/editorial: "We've been thinking about..." / "Here's what we found..."

**2. Lead with the feeling, follow with the fact.**
Every paragraph should open with an emotional or sensory observation, then ground it with a specific detail. Never the reverse.

**3. Use short sentences when you want something to land.**
Long sentences build atmosphere. Short sentences create impact. Know which you need.

**4. Never use these words:** magical, stunning, breathtaking, life-changing, perfect, amazing, incredible, unforgettable (unless used ironically), wanderlust, cozy (overused to meaninglessness), hidden gem, bucket list (unless subverting it).

**5. Earn the superlative.** If you're going to say something is the best, the most unique, or unlike anything else — prove it in the sentence before. The claim should feel inevitable by the time you make it, not like an assertion you're hoping the reader accepts.

**6. End with an action or an open door.** Every caption, every paragraph, every newsletter section should close with either a specific action ("Link in bio to book") or an open question that keeps the reader thinking ("What would you do with three days and no agenda?").

**7. Read it aloud before publishing.** If you stumble on a sentence, rewrite it. If it sounds like it was written by a committee, rewrite it. If it sounds like a press release, delete it and start over.

### Rules We Never Break

- Never write copy that implies a stay is valuable primarily because it photographs well.
- Never use exclamation points in listing descriptions. (Sparingly acceptable in Stories and casual captions only.)
- Never write "perfect for" — it's lazy and presumptuous. Show, don't tell.
- Never start a sentence with "Whether you're..." — it's the most overused construction in travel writing.
- Never use passive voice in headlines or opening sentences.
- Never describe a price as "affordable" or "budget-friendly" — these are relative and meaningless. Give the actual number.

## Part IV: Platform-Specific Adaptations

### Instagram Feed Captions

**Length:** 150–300 words. Long enough to tell a story. Short enough to be read in full.

**Structure:**
1. Hook (1–2 sentences) — The feeling or the specific detail that stops the scroll
2. The story (3–5 sentences) — What makes this place worth knowing about
3. The stoic beat (1–2 sentences, optional) — The philosophical undercurrent
4. The CTA (1 sentence) — Direct, specific, no fluff

**Caption Framework — "The Reveal":**
> [Surprising or counterintuitive opening]
> [Specific detail that earns the opening]
> [The feeling of being there]
> [One practical fact: price, location, or platform]
> [CTA]

**Example:**
> Who knew an igloo underneath the northern lights would make you rethink everything?
>
> This one is in Montana — not Iceland, not Norway. Built by hand by a former architect who left his firm in 2019 and spent two years figuring out how to make a structure that disappears into the snow. The walls are thick enough to keep you warm at -20°F. The ceiling is a skylight. You do the math.
>
> There's something about lying flat on your back watching the sky move that makes the things you were worried about last week seem appropriately sized.
>
> $340/night on Airbnb. Link in bio.

### Instagram Reels Scripts

**Format:** Hook in first 2 seconds. No intro. No "hey guys." No preamble.

**The "You Didn't Know" Framework:**
- Second 0–2: The visual hook (the most striking shot of the property)
- Second 2–5: Text overlay — "You can actually rent this [property type] in [state]"
- Second 5–15: Quick cuts of the best 4–5 features, each with a one-line text overlay
- Second 15–25: The feeling shot — the view, the quiet moment, the detail that makes it
- Second 25–30: CTA — "Link in bio to book" or "Save this for your next trip"

**Voiceover tone (if used):** Conversational, slightly dry, never breathless. Think Tim Ferriss podcast intro, not travel vlog.

### Newsletter — "The Wanderer's Weekly"

**Voice:** Slightly more personal than Instagram. This is where the first-person plural "we" lives most comfortably. The newsletter reader has opted in — they want more, not less.

**Opening paragraph rule:** Never start with "This week we're covering..." Start with a specific scene, a question, or an observation that earns the reader's attention before you tell them what the issue is about.

**The city guide structure:**
1. The opening scene (2–3 sentences that put the reader in the city)
2. Why this city, why now (1 paragraph — the editorial argument)
3. The stays (3–5 listings, each with the feeling-first description)
4. What to do (3–5 specific recommendations — not "visit the museum," but which museum and what specifically to see there)
5. The closer (1 paragraph — a reflection, a question, or a stoic observation that gives the issue a sense of completion)

**Subject line formula:** Specific + Intriguing, never clickbait.
- Good: "The lighthouse in Maine you can actually rent"
- Good: "Asheville, but make it weird"
- Good: "Why we keep coming back to the Pacific Northwest"
- Bad: "You won't believe this unique stay 😱"
- Bad: "Top 10 unique stays this week!"

### Website Listing Descriptions

**Length:** 80–120 words per listing. Tight. Every word earns its place.

**Structure:**
1. The opening image (1–2 sentences, feeling-first)
2. The specific detail that makes it unique (1–2 sentences)
3. The practical anchor (1 sentence — platform, price range, location)
4. The implicit invitation (1 sentence — not a CTA, but a closing image that leaves the reader wanting to click)

## Part V: What We Sound Like vs. What We Don't

### Side-by-Side Comparisons

| Generic Travel Voice | UniqueStaysUSA Voice |
|---|---|
| "Stunning treehouse with breathtaking views" | "Built into a 200-year-old Douglas fir. The kind of tree that makes you feel appropriately small." |
| "Perfect romantic getaway for couples" | "Two people, no agenda, a hot tub overlooking the Cascades. You'll figure out the rest." |
| "Cozy cabin in the mountains" | "The nearest town is 40 minutes away. That's not a warning — that's the point." |
| "Unique and unforgettable experience" | "You'll tell this story at dinner parties for years. Not because it was luxurious — because it was specific." |
| "Book now before it's too late!" | "Available most weekends through October. Link in bio." |
| "This hidden gem is a must-visit!" | "It's not hidden — it's just not obvious. There's a difference." |

### Brands We Sound Like (in different ways)

**Mark Manson:** The directness. The willingness to say "here's what I actually think" without hedging. The dry humor that emerges from honesty rather than effort.

**Tim Ferriss:** The specificity. The "I've done the research so you don't have to" positioning. The efficiency of language — no word that doesn't earn its place.

**Nomadlist:** The data-informed confidence. The lack of sentimentality. The sense that the person writing has genuinely thought about this, not just assembled information.

**Kinfolk Magazine:** The visual restraint. The belief that a single specific detail is worth more than a paragraph of adjectives. The quiet confidence that the work speaks for itself.

### Brands We Do Not Sound Like

**Generic travel blogs:** Excessive superlatives, no point of view, "whether you're a solo traveler or a family of four" hedging.

**Luxury hotel marketing:** Aspirational distance, passive voice, language designed to make you feel like you're not quite good enough yet.

**Travel influencer captions:** Performative enthusiasm, exclamation points, the implication that the value of a place is proportional to how good it looks in photos.

**AI-generated content:** Smooth but hollow. Technically correct but emotionally absent. The kind of writing that answers the question without ever having understood why it was asked.

## Part VI: The Editorial Persona

### The Voice Behind the "We"

UniqueStaysUSA speaks in a consistent first-person plural — "we found," "we think," "here's what we noticed." This is not a corporate "we." It is the "we" of a small, opinionated team of people who have done the work of curation and are now sharing what they found.

The implied narrator is:
- Someone who has traveled enough to have taste, but not so much that they've become jaded
- Someone who reads — not just travel content, but philosophy, history, long-form journalism
- Someone who values the quality of an experience over the quantity of experiences
- Someone who is comfortable saying "this isn't for everyone" because they know exactly who it is for

This narrator does not need to have personally visited every listing. They have done the research. They have read the reviews. They have looked at the photos carefully. They have thought about what kind of person would love this place and why. That is a form of expertise — curatorial expertise — and it is worth owning.

### On Authenticity

The greatest risk for this brand is sounding like it was written by an algorithm. The antidote is not trying harder to sound human — it is being specific enough that an algorithm couldn't have written it.

An algorithm can write "beautiful treehouse with stunning views." It cannot write "the kind of quiet you only find in old-growth forests, where the wind stays up in the canopy and the ground is almost completely still."

Specificity is the proof of presence. Write like someone who was actually paying attention.

## Part VII: Quick Reference

### The Brand Voice in Five Words
**Grounded. Adventurous. Specific. Witty. Present.**

### The Single Question to Ask Before Publishing
*Does this make someone feel "I need to go there"?*

### Words We Use
curious, specific, particular, worth it, the kind of place, you'll notice, there's something about, earned, quiet, unhurried, deliberate, tucked, the sort of thing, real, actual, honest

### Words We Avoid
stunning, breathtaking, magical, life-changing, perfect, amazing, incredible, unforgettable, cozy (overused), hidden gem, wanderlust, bucket list, must-see, don't miss, game-changer, unique (ironic given our name — describe *why* it's unique instead)

### The Stoic Test
Before publishing, ask: *Does this copy respect the reader's intelligence and their time?* If it oversells, hedges, or performs enthusiasm it doesn't feel, rewrite it.

### The Irish Storytelling Test
Before publishing, ask: *Is there at least one specific detail in here that could only apply to this place?* If not, add one.
